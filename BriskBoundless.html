From Brisk Entrances to Boundless Stars

A Journey Toward Inner Freedom

Today, I want to share a story, a story not just about survival, but about the quiet triumph of the human spirit, and the sacred journey toward finally feeling free to be myself.

For most of my life, I carried a deep longing, not to impress, not to succeed, but simply to feel safe being me, without the pressure to perform or please. I dreamed of a world where I didn’t have to bring my ego to meet someone else’s. A world where connection wasn’t a transaction, but a gentle meeting of souls.

But for years, I was on high alert. My nervous system lived in a state of bracing: watchful, calculating, always accommodating. I moved through public spaces with what I called a “brisk” entrance, quick, deliberate, with my awareness tuned outward. Not because I was confident, but because I had to be ready. Ready for someone to come at me sideways. Ready to defend, deflect, or disappear.

When I was focused, it felt like a soft beam of light in front of me, like vintage VW Beetle headlights on a dark road. But if something startled me from the side, out of nowhere, that focus would vanish. My beam would collapse into a full-body radar, spinning wildly in every direction. Frantic. Overwhelmed. Scrambled.

It felt like radio static between my ears, like tinnitus. My body would tighten, especially high inside my left leg, as if scar tissue had formed around old wounds. All I wanted in those moments was for it to stop, for the tension to melt and my system to cool, like a snowflake landing on warm skin.

I’d close my eyes and imagine a peppermint winter, icy white and peaceful, somewhere deep in the wilds of Alaska or Canada. I saw a bobcat and mountain lion curled up together in safety, like a mother and her child. In that vision, nothing had to be done. Nothing had to be said. I could simply be.

That longing for peace was rooted in something that happened long ago, a moment that shaped everything.

I was just a child, sitting in the back of our old VW Beetle, when my father accidentally left it in neutral on a hill. It began to roll toward a lake. My dog was next to me. And in that split second, if the wheel hadn’t been turned just so, we would’ve gone under.

Time stopped. I didn’t scream. I didn’t think. I surrendered to death.

That moment changed me. It drained me of dignity, of safety, of childhood. It was as if my soul slipped through the cracks in the floorboards, like a spilled drink disappearing through old wood. After that, I never felt safe with my father. Not physically. Not emotionally. I learned to monitor his moods like a radar tech during a storm. Was he silly? Was he angry? Was he about to explode?

And I loved him. That was the hardest part. I told myself, Someone has to help him. So I protected his feelings instead of mine.

He could flip like a switch, from fury to laughter. From rage to play. It was confusing. Heartbreaking. But I remember those rare moments when his heart softened, it was like his soul plugged into the old TV set with rabbit ears in our living room. That TV… it was magical. The static between the channels mirrored the chaos in my mind, but when it cleared, it felt like my whole being had exhaled. The buzzing stopped. I could breathe. I could rest.

It was like being carried to bed after falling asleep in the car: safe, held, human again.

Over time, I learned something remarkable.

That old “surrender,” the one I once thought meant defeat or death, was actually a doorway. A doorway to something new. A different kind of surrender. One that wasn’t about giving up, but about letting go.

Letting go of the need to scan the room.

Letting go of the need to brace for impact.

Letting go of the belief that I had to protect everyone but me.

And in that letting go, I found something precious.

Relief.

·

Safety.

·

Ecstasy.

I could breathe again. A deep, full breath that spread through my body like warm sunlight on a frozen lake. My sense of self expanded, not just in thought, but in feeling. I imagined myself as a giant bubble, soft and alive, reaching out to the edge of the solar system, brushing against stars, open to the cosmos.

This journey, from contraction to expansion, from protection to presence, is still unfolding. But what I know now is this:

We are not our vigilance.

We are not our trauma.

We are not the pain we inherited from those who didn't know how to love us safely.

We are light.

We are breath.

We are beings capable of expanding past the limits we once thought were permanent.

There is a peace waiting for you, too. Not out there, but within. And when you find it, you’ll remember: you were born for this freedom.
